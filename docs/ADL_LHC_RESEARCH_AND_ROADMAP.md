# State of ADL at the LHC — an Honest Brief

## 1. What ADL/CutLang is, and who maintains it

The Analysis Description Language (ADL) is a declarative, domain-specific language for expressing the physics content of an LHC analysis — object/variable definitions, event selection, binning, histogramming, and event counts — in a framework-independent way. Its grammar is a fixed set of blocks and keywords (`object`/`obj`; `region` with `select`/`reject`/`weight`/`bin`/`bins`/`sort`/`counts`/`histo`/`save`), documented on the CERN ADL TWiki (https://twiki.cern.ch/twiki/bin/view/LHCPhysics/ADL).

ADL's design principles were first discussed at the Les Houches PhysTeV 2015 workshop, where the prototype LHADA (Les Houches Analysis Description Accord) was drafted (Les Houches 2015 New Physics WG report, https://arxiv.org/pdf/1605.02684, Sec. 16-17). In 2019, LHADA and the separately-developed CutLang language were merged into the unified ADL, at the first dedicated "Analysis Description Languages for the LHC" workshop at **Fermilab LPC, 6-8 May 2019** (https://indico.cern.ch/event/769263/; ecosystem report https://arxiv.org/abs/2011.01950). Note: some secondary write-ups misattribute this workshop to CERN — it was Fermilab.

**CutLang** is the canonical runtime interpreter for ADL: written in C++, built on CERN's ROOT, parsing ADL via Lex & Yacc. Original paper: S. Sekmen and G. Unel, *CutLang: A Particle Physics Analysis Description Language and Runtime Interpreter*, Comput. Phys. Commun. 233 (2018) 215-236 (https://arxiv.org/abs/1801.05727); current generation is CutLang V3 (https://github.com/unelg/CutLang). (Note: Semantic Scholar reverses the author order; the published order is Sekmen & Unel.)

**Maintainers / governance.** Principal developers are Gokhan Unel (UC Irvine, principal CutLang developer) and Sezen Sekmen (Kyungpook National University, ADL syntax designer), plus a rotating set of students. CutLang lives at https://github.com/unelg/CutLang; the ADL analysis database at https://github.com/ADL4HEP/ADLLHCanalyses. This is an **informal community/academic effort with no formal standards body** — the language is explicitly unversioned and "develops further as we implement more analyses." The de-facto spec is the CERN TWiki plus CutLang's own parser behavior; documentation portal at cern.ch/adl → adl.web.cern.ch (https://conference.ippp.dur.ac.uk/event/1178/contributions/6466/attachments/5030/6425/SekmenADLCLreint2308.pdf).

## 2. Actual adoption — candid

**ADL/CutLang is at the validation/demonstration stage, not in production.** No official ATLAS or CMS publication describes its event selection natively in ADL; the analyses that exist in ADL are community re-implementations of already-published searches. Be explicit about the three places it has genuine, documented use:

- **Reinterpretation/recasting** is the flagship demonstrated capability, notably via the SModelS Efficiency-Map Creator, adapted to work with ADL/CutLang (Sekmen 2023 RIF talk, link above). A "large-scale analysis (re)implementation and validation effort" was launched, working with ATLAS/CMS students and *partially* with CMS analysis teams — but validation of recent analyses is repeatedly described as "in progress"/"ongoing."
- **Education** is the most concrete *recurring* use: the Istanbul "Computing Applications in Particle Physics" school (5th ed., Feb 2020, 30 students) had teams reproduce assigned ATLAS/CMS analyses in CutLang in ~1.5 days (https://arxiv.org/abs/2008.12034); use at the 26th Vietnam School of Physics (Dec 2020) via Jupyter; and an official lesson in the CMS Open Data Workshop 2023 reinterpretation track (https://cms-opendata-workshop.github.io/workshop2023-lesson-reinterpretation/02-adlclintro/index.html).
- **Open-data reproduction**, framed by the developers themselves as validation exercises, cross-checked against other frameworks at Les Houches PhysTeV workshops (CutLang v2 paper, Front. Big Data 2021, https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/big-data/articles/10.3389/fdata.2021.659986/full).

**Scale, candidly.** The entire public ADL corpus (ADL4HEP/ADLLHCanalyses) is **~16 analyses — 15 CMS + 1 ATLAS, overwhelmingly CMS SUSY** (plus one Higgs, CMS-OD-12350-Htautau, and one ATLAS exotica, ATLAS-EXOT-2016-32). The repo has 1 GitHub star, 1 fork, and was last pushed 2023-05-09. (Caution: a widely-circulated figure of "~19 / 18 CMS" is an overcount — the verified directory count is 16; the inflation likely comes from counting per-backend `.adl` variants within a directory as separate analyses.) Community uptake is, by these metrics, near-zero outside the developer circle.

The 2025 CERN-LPCC reinterpretation review gives ADL a single sentence — "recently seen some traction within CMS" — and, unlike Rivet/MadAnalysis5/CheckMATE, **no validation status, deployment, or quantitative adoption metrics** (https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.00256).

## 3. Where ADL sits vs Rivet / MadAnalysis5 / SModelS / pyhf

The LHC Reinterpretation Forum's Run-2 report (SciPost Phys. 9, 022, 2020; https://arxiv.org/abs/2003.07868) frames two reinterpretation *approaches* (the brief's "two-tier" phrasing is a paraphrase, not the report's term):

- **Simplified-model recasting** — fast, generic, less precise: **SModelS**.
- **Full Monte-Carlo recasting** — more general and precise, slower: **CheckMATE, MadAnalysis 5, GAMBIT ColliderBit, Rivet (and hence Contur)** — and, introduced more softly ("there are also two interpreters of the recently developed ADL"), **adl2tnm and CutLang**.

So ADL/CutLang sits in the **full-recast tier**, but as a newer, less-established entrant than the named incumbents. Statistics is a **separate layer**: none of these tools do inference themselves; the community standard is **pyhf/HistFactory JSON likelihoods published on HEPData**, with **HS3** as the emerging cross-tool serialization standard (https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.00256; HS3 paper https://arxiv.org/html/2606.01760). ADL/CutLang explicitly does **not** perform statistical inference, hypothesis testing, or detector simulation — it reads detector-level input (Delphes, NanoAOD, open data) and emits counts/histograms for a downstream stats layer.

The contrast in adoption is instructive: public likelihoods went from a 2019 novelty to **23 ATLAS full models on HEPData by 2021** and **~41 statistical-model hits (16 SUSY + 21 top) by the 2025 review**, with SModelS and MadAnalysis5 building pyhf interfaces (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2211.15838; https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.01809). That is real momentum — though the same 2025 review stresses coverage "remains sparse" and still *urges* collaborations to make this standard, so it is **established-and-growing, not yet near-standard**. ADL has not seen comparable uptake.

Two corrections worth flagging for honesty:
- The recurring claim that "CutLang is faster than adl2tnm" is **inverted**. Primary sources say CutLang the interpreter is **~20% slower** than the compiled adl2tnm-generated C++ (Front. Big Data 2021; CHEP 2021, https://arxiv.org/abs/2108.00857). ADL tooling should not compete on raw execution speed.
- ATLAS **SimpleAnalysis** (published via HEPData as pseudo-code documentation) and **Rivet** are the de-facto incumbent preservation/recast formats to interoperate with — not displace (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2312.14575).

## 4. Momentum and direction (2023-2026)

- **2023:** active CutLang releases (v2.14.2, May 2023); the launch of the large-scale reimplementation/validation effort; inclusion in the official CMS Open Data Workshop. But the public analysis corpus stops being updated (last push May 2023).
- **2024-2025:** the most recent status paper, *The Analysis Description Language Ecosystem* (PoS(ICHEP2024)1056, DOI 10.22323/1.476.1056, published early-to-mid 2025; https://pos.sissa.it/476/1056/), reports a newly automated **AST generation** "serving as a foundation for various future applications," and a flowchart **visualization tool, adl2flowchart** (cited as ref [4], by D. Riley). It states, verbatim, that "**ongoing work aims to leverage static analysis techniques to automate queries across multiple analyses, identify overlaps and disjoint analyses, and assist in performing combination studies.**"
- **Direction:** the trajectory is *away from* competing on execution and *toward* static analysis of the ADL AST — cross-analysis querying, overlap/disjointness detection, and combination support.

**Honest caveat on this direction:** it is not a freshly-minted 2024 roadmap. The same overlap/disjoint/combination aspiration — including a prototype Python sampling script — was published by the same group in the **Les Houches 2019 report** (https://arxiv.org/abs/2002.12220, Sec. 3.1-3.2), and the 2025 paper restates it as still "ongoing." **It has been a stated-but-unrealized goal for ~5 years.** What exists in the canonical chain today is at most *sampling/scan-based overlap detection* (described in the 2022 democratization paper, https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.13302), not a logical proof. There is **no sound, solver-backed region-relation proof tool** anywhere in the ADL ecosystem, and the developers state plainly that CutLang does not check inter-region consistency — "Ensuring the consistency of the algorithm needs to be done by the user," and only *bins within a region* are non-overlapping by definition (Front. Big Data 2021).

## 5. Needs and gaps an ADL tool could own

The recurring theme across the ecosystem's own documents: **ADL tools execute selections; nothing in the chain reasons about relations between regions or across analyses.** That is the gap with the strongest documentary support and the least competition.

Ranked, each tagged with how well-evidenced it is:

- **[STRONGLY EVIDENCED] Sound cross-analysis overlap / disjointness / subset reasoning over the AST.** Explicitly named as a goal in the 2020 ecosystem report, the 2019 Les Houches report, the 2023 RIF talk, and the 2025 PoS(ICHEP2024)1056 paper — and *never implemented as a proof*. Only sampling-based detection exists. This is the single best-evidenced, uncontested gap.
- **[STRONGLY EVIDENCED] Combination-study support — which regions can be statistically combined (i.e., are provably disjoint).** Named alongside overlap detection in the same official sources; this is the "killer application" framing, and the precondition a correct multi-channel pyhf/HS3 likelihood needs. No ADL tool provides it.
- **[STRONGLY EVIDENCED] A deterministic, framework-independent reference interpreter for cross-framework cutflow validation.** The credibility currency in this community is numeric cutflow agreement (Les Houches PhysTeV exercises); ADL/CutLang's own validation is repeatedly "ongoing." A provably-deterministic reference interpreter directly addresses the credibility gap that holds ADL back.
- **[WELL EVIDENCED] AST-based visualization / flowcharts of analysis structure.** The community is actively building exactly this (adl2flowchart, cited in PoS(ICHEP2024)1056); pedagogically valuable given that education is ADL's most concrete user base. Strong fit, but already partially occupied by the community's own effort.
- **[WELL EVIDENCED] Verified bin-partition coverage (not merely assumed).** CutLang treats intra-region bins as non-overlapping *by definition*; actually *verifying* partition/coverage is a strictly stronger guarantee nothing in the chain offers. Evidenced by absence in CutLang docs/TWiki.
- **[MODERATELY EVIDENCED] CMS NanoAOD / open-data ntuple ingestion.** CutLang's real adoption surface is open data, CMS NanoAOD, Delphes, and LHCO (Front. Big Data 2021). A NanoAOD/open-data path is where the actual ADL user base lives; a Delphes-only tool misses it. Evidence is solid on *where users are*, inferential on the size of the resulting adoption gain.
- **[MODERATELY EVIDENCED] Clear parse/resolve diagnostics for teaching.** Education is the documented recurring use; good diagnostics meet that audience. Defensible from the education evidence, but no source explicitly names diagnostics as a gap.
- **[SPECULATIVE] Emitting per-region/per-bin counts as pyhf/HS3-consumable artifacts.** The ecosystem's center of gravity is moving to serialized, reusable statistical artifacts. A natural bridge — and disjointness proofs are the precondition for a correct multi-channel likelihood — but no source states this integration as a community ask; treat as a forward bet, not a documented need.

**Bottom line:** ADL/CutLang is a real, actively-developed, but **niche reinterpretation-and-education tool**, not an experiment-mandated or production standard, with a tiny (~16-analysis) public corpus and near-zero external uptake. Its honest opportunity is not to out-execute Rivet/MadAnalysis5/CheckMATE or to do statistics (that is pyhf/HS3 territory), but to **own the sound region-relation / overlap / combination layer the community has wished for since 2019 and still has not built** — and to back it with deterministic, validatable cutflow semantics.

## Executive summary

ADL at the LHC is, in practice, a *preservation and recasting* notation: its real users live on CMS Open Data / NanoAOD and Delphes (CutLang's documented adoption surface; CMS Open Data Workshop 2023 reinterpretation lesson), and the ecosystem's center of gravity for the statistics layer it feeds is pyhf/HistFactory JSON on HEPData, with HS3 emerging as the cross-tool serialization standard. Every ADL tool to date stops at *executing selections and emitting counts* and explicitly leaves inter-region/inter-analysis consistency to the user (CutLang does not check it; the gap has been named in the 2019 Les Houches report, the 2020 ecosystem report, the 2023 RIF talk, and PoS(ICHEP2024)1056 — and never built as a proof). smash2's defensible niche is therefore **not** to be a faster executor but to be the *trustworthy reference-semantics layer*: the only ADL tool that (a) produces a byte-deterministic reference cutflow and (b) emits sound, solver-backed PROVEN DISJOINT / OVERLAPPING / SUBSET verdicts with re-validated witnesses. The roadmap's thesis: pay down the one-file limitation that blocks the proving differentiator, get onto NanoAOD where the users actually are, and ship the cheap tooling/docs that make the differentiator visible — interop and proof, not raw execution speed.

## The big bet

**Bet on the cross-analysis proof stack (N1 → X1 → X2 → X3): become the ADL ecosystem's combination/overlap-soundness layer.** This is the highest-leverage direction because it is the intersection of three facts: it is the best-evidenced, longest-standing, still-unfilled community ask (2019→2024); it is something *no other tool can do soundly* (CutLang explicitly punts; everyone else samples); and the codebase was deliberately architected for it (structural-not-string identity in quantity.rs, a unit-agnostic `pair()` engine, witness re-validation, unsat-core-to-span mapping). The single readiness gap is multi-file plumbing — an L-sized extension of an existing design, not a rewrite. If smash2 owns "which preserved analyses' regions are provably disjoint and therefore safely combinable, with a witness when they are not," it occupies a niche the rest of the ecosystem structurally cannot, and it becomes the natural front-end to the pyhf/HS3 combination workflows the field is converging on.

**The honest case against it:** the *audience* for sound cross-analysis combination proofs is small and expert — global-fit conveners and recasters at ATLAS/CMS — not the schools and open-data learners who are ADL's largest, most concrete user base. There is a real risk of building a rigorous answer to a question only a dozen people are actively asking, while the broader adoption blocker (you can't even read a NanoAOD file, X4) goes unaddressed. The pragmatic counter-bet is X4 + N2/N3 + L1: meet users where they are (NanoAOD), give them fast feedback and docs, and earn credibility with a published cutflow-agreement table. The roadmap hedges by putting the cheap adoption items in **Now** alongside the keystone, so the big bet does not starve adoption — but if forced to pick *one* identity for the tool, the defensible, uncontested one is the proof layer.

## Explicitly out of scope / risks

**Do not build:**
- **A faster general-purpose ADL *executor*.** Competing with CutLang on raw execution speed forfeits the only differentiator. The interpreter's job is to be the *correct, deterministic oracle*, not the fastest runner (this is why L7 columnar is gated behind the oracle and ranked last).
- **An in-tool statistics/inference layer (running fits, computing limits).** The ecosystem already has pyhf/cabinetry/SModelS. smash2 should emit the *input artifact* with a soundness certificate (L2/L3) and stop at the handoff. Crossing into inference duplicates mature tools and dilutes the niche.
- **Speculative expressiveness for ML/imaginary observables** beyond what the public corpus actually uses. Every construct added to the checked fragment is a soundness liability (L6's constraint).
- **A heavy IDE/GUI.** A thin LSP (L5) is the ceiling; anything more is effort the small user base won't repay.

**Risks / assumptions:**
- **Soundness is the whole franchise (highest risk).** Per the adl2-soundness contract, a single wrong fact on the UNSAT side fabricates a false PROVEN with no safety net. The cross-file merge (N1) and object reconciliation (X2) are *exactly* where a careless identity merge could silently fabricate a false disjointness verdict across analyses. X2 is not optional polish — it is the soundness gate that makes X1/X3 trustworthy, and it must land *with* X1, not after.
- **pyhf/HS3 demand is inferred, not requested** — L2/L3 are deliberately Later; validate demand (talk to one recasting group) before investing.
